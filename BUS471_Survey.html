BREAKING: Veridian Technologies Hit by Massive Data Breach
Technology Business Journal — February 12, 2026

Veridian Technologies, a major enterprise software company headquartered in San Francisco, confirmed today that it suffered a significant cybersecurity breach that exposed the personal data of approximately 5 million customers worldwide. The breach, which was discovered by the company’s internal security team on January 28, went undetected for nearly two weeks before being publicly disclosed.

According to Veridian’s Chief Information Security Officer, the compromised data includes customers’ full names, email addresses, phone numbers, and financial information including credit card numbers and bank account details. The company has confirmed that the breach originated from a vulnerability in its cloud storage infrastructure that was exploited by an external attacker.

Veridian, which reported $3.2 billion in annual revenue last year, has offered affected customers free credit monitoring services for 24 months. The company faces potential regulatory fines under data protection laws in multiple jurisdictions. Cybersecurity analysts estimate the total cost of the breach could exceed $400 million, including legal fees, remediation, and reputational damage.

            Thank you for completing this study!

            This study examined whether a dramatic corporate announcement can redirect people’s attention away from a prior negative event. The company “Veridian Technologies” and all news articles were entirely fictional and created solely for this research.

            You were randomly assigned to read either a neutral follow-up article or a dramatic acquisition announcement after the initial breach article. We are studying whether the type of follow-up information affects how well people remember and how concerned they feel about the original event.

            This research is based on Daniel Kahneman’s theory of limited attention capacity, which suggests that people can only focus on a limited amount of information at any given time. Understanding how attention works in the context of corporate communications has important implications for strategic management and organizational behavior.

            Your responses are anonymous and will be used only for academic purposes. If you have any questions or concerns about this study, please contact James Liang at yla692@sfu.ca.
